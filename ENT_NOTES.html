Chapter 1 — Introduction to Entrepreneurship

What Is Entrepreneurship and Why Is It Important?

Core Definitions

Entrepreneur A person who assembles and integrates all the resources needed — money, people, business model, strategy, and risk-bearing ability — to transform an invention or idea into a viable business.
Entrepreneurship The process by which individuals pursue opportunities without regard to resources they currently control for the purpose of exploiting future goods and services. Simply: the art of turning an idea into a business.
Key Distinction

An inventor creates something new. An entrepreneur builds a business around it. The entrepreneur's job is to recognise an opportunity, gather resources, manage risk, and execute.

Corporate Entrepreneurship Established firms that practice entrepreneurial behaviors — proactive, innovative, and risk-taking — within an existing organisation (e.g., Google, Apple).
Entrepreneurial Intensity A firm's position on a spectrum from "highly conservative" (wait-and-see, risk-averse, less innovative) to "highly entrepreneurial" (proactive, innovative, willing to take calculated risks).

Why Do People Become Entrepreneurs? (3 Reasons)

① Be Their Own Boss

The most commonly cited reason. Entrepreneurs often feel confined by corporate structures, frustrated by bureaucracy, or have always desired independence. Some transition gradually — starting part-time — to test the waters before leaving stable employment.

Example Wendy DeFeudis started VeryWendy (customised invitations) because she found corporate life "a complete waste of time" — too many meetings, too many approvals before acting.

② Pursue Their Own Ideas

Many entrepreneurs are naturally alert to problems or opportunities. When a corporation resists innovation, passionate employees leave and build their own venture. Ideas may also emerge from hobbies or everyday life.

Example Marco Zappacosta founded Thumbtack after seeing that local service providers (plumbers, teachers, etc.) couldn't easily find clients, and ordinary people couldn't find trustworthy providers.

③ Pursue Financial Rewards

Though real, this motivation is usually secondary. The average entrepreneur does not earn more than a manager in a traditional job. The financial upside potential (think Bezos, Zuckerberg) is the lure, not guaranteed returns. Money can even feel bittersweet (e.g., Branson cried when he sold Virgin Records for $1 billion).

Exam Tip: In a scenario, look for which of these three motivations drives the character. Also note: passion must be for the business itself, not just for "being an entrepreneur" — as the Prim laundry case demonstrates (founders weren't passionate about laundry, only about being connectors → failed).

Characteristics of Successful Entrepreneurs

Research identifies four primary characteristics shared by successful entrepreneurs:

① Passion for the Business

The #1 characteristic. Passion drives learning, sustains hard work, fuels recovery from failure, enables listening to feedback, and provides perseverance. Without passion for the specific business (not just entrepreneurship in general), the venture is fragile.

Important

Passion ≠ naivety. Entrepreneurs should be enthusiastic but must understand their idea's flaws and risks. Rose-coloured glasses are dangerous. The best ideas arise where passion, skills, and a real business opportunity overlap.

② Product/Customer Focus

Successful entrepreneurs maintain an intense focus on building great products AND understanding what customers truly need. This focus involves spotting product opportunities and seeing them through to completion.

Key insight: None of the management, marketing, or finance work matters if the firm does not have good products that satisfy customers.

③ Tenacity Despite Failure

New businesses require experimentation — like a scientist trying combinations. Failure is part of the process. The litmus test is the entrepreneur's ability to persevere. Yelp's pivot from an email-request model to a review platform after its initial failure is a classic example.

④ Execution Intelligence

The ability to translate a solid idea into a viable, operational business. This includes building a team, raising money, managing finances, forming partnerships, and converting creativity into measurable results.

Jeff Bezos: "Ideas are easy. It's execution that's hard." An ancient Chinese saying warns: "To open a business is very easy; to keep it open is very difficult."

Common Traits of Entrepreneurs (Table from book):

  • Moderate risk taker (NOT a gambler)
  • Achievement motivated, self-confident, decisive
  • Alert to opportunities, creative, visionary
  • Self-starter, energetic, tenacious
  • Tolerant of ambiguity, optimistic, persuasive
  • Networker, resource assembler, strong work ethic

5 Common Myths About Entrepreneurs

MythReality
1. Born, Not MadeNo genetic predisposition. Environment, life experiences, and personal choices shape entrepreneurs. Having entrepreneur parents or role models helps, but anyone can develop entrepreneurial traits.
2. GamblersEntrepreneurs are moderate risk takers. 79% of small business owners in one study rated themselves conservative, not risky.
3. Motivated by MoneyMoney is usually secondary. Average entrepreneurs don't earn more than managers. The lure is upside potential. Building the business itself is often the real motivation.
4. Must Be Young & EnergeticBusiness ownership is evenly distributed across ages 20–64. Experience, reputation, and track record — qualities of older entrepreneurs — actually matter more to investors.
5. Love the SpotlightMost entrepreneurs work quietly and avoid public attention, especially when developing proprietary products. Very few become household names.

Types of Start-Up Firms

TypeDescriptionExample
Salary-Substitute Firms Yield similar income to a traditional job. Offer common, non-innovative products/services. The vast majority of small businesses. Dry cleaners, convenience stores, accounting firms, hair salons
Lifestyle Firms Allow owners to pursue a specific lifestyle or passion. Not innovative, grow slowly, often employ very few people. Golf pros, bed & breakfasts, tour guides, personal trainers
Entrepreneurial Firms Bring new products and services to market by creating and seizing opportunities. Create real value for customers. Often grow rapidly. Dropbox, Facebook, Uber
Key Point for Scenarios

When reading a scenario, determine: Is this person creating something genuinely new (entrepreneurial firm) or simply replicating an existing formula (salary-substitute)? This affects how the Barringer/Ireland template applies.

Changing Demographics of Entrepreneurs

  • Women Entrepreneurs: Number of women-owned businesses is rising globally. In developed economies, access to capital, training, and markets makes starting easier. In developing economies, necessity-driven entrepreneurship is common. Women make up 34.4% of EU's self-employed.
  • Minority Entrepreneurs: Growing rapidly. Over 4 million minority-owned companies in the US with ~$700 billion in annual sales (2020).
  • Senior Entrepreneurs: Many seniors have deep experience, financial resources, and energy. Corporate downsizing, desire for fulfilment, and health-care cost concerns drive this group.
  • Millennial Entrepreneurs: Started businesses at younger ages (average: 22 vs 35 for Gen X). More likely to innovate, use e-commerce, and create jobs. 62% of millennials globally intended to start a business before 2026 (GoDaddy survey).

Positive Effects of Entrepreneurship & Entrepreneurial Firms

On the Economy

  • Innovation: Small innovative firms are 16× more productive than large innovative firms in patents per employee. Creative destruction (Schumpeter, 1934) — new products replace old ones, stimulating economic activity.
  • Job Creation: SMEs create 85% of all new EU jobs. Companies less than 1 year old create ~1.5 million jobs/year in the US (Kauffman Foundation).

On Society & Larger Firms

Innovations improve health care, productivity, entertainment, and quality of life. Entrepreneurial firms also supply component parts and services that make large firms more effective.

The Entrepreneurial Process (4 Steps)

4-Step Entrepreneurial Process
1
Decision to Become an Entrepreneur Usually triggered by an event: job loss, an inheritance, a lifestyle change, frustration at work. The person decides to start a business.
2
Developing Successful Business Ideas Opportunity recognition → Feasibility analysis → Business model development → Industry analysis → Business plan writing.
3
Moving from an Idea to an Entrepreneurial Firm Legal/ethical prep, choosing a business structure, assessing new venture teams, raising money.
4
Managing and Growing an Entrepreneurial Firm Marketing, intellectual property, managing growth, franchising, etc.
Chapter 2 — Recognizing Opportunities and Generating Ideas

Opportunities vs. Ideas & How to Find Both

The Critical Difference: Opportunity vs. Idea

Idea A thought, an impression, or a notion. An idea may or may not represent a real opportunity. Many businesses fail because there was no real opportunity to begin with — just an idea.
Opportunity A favorable set of circumstances that creates a need for a new product, service, or business. An opportunity has four essential qualities:
4 Essential Qualities of an Opportunity

① Attractive — has market demand
② Timely — the window of opportunity is open
③ Durable — sustainable, not just a fad
④ Anchored in Value — creates or adds real value for buyers/users

Window of Opportunity The time period during which a firm can realistically enter a new market. Opens when a new market forms; closes when the market matures and becomes saturated. Example: Internet search engines — open (1995–2005), now effectively closed for new entrants.
Opportunity Gap The difference between what is currently available and what is possible — the space where new businesses can enter.

Common mistake: Picking a product you are passionate about and trying to build a slightly better version of it. The key is finding something people need and will buy, not something an entrepreneur wants to make and sell.

Three Ways to Identify Opportunities

① Observing Trends

Monitor changes in the environment. Four types of trends to watch:

Trend TypeWhat to Look ForBusiness Examples
Economic Forces Rising/falling incomes, housing starts, interest rates, who has disposable income (women in workforce, baby boomers, minorities) SoulCycle (rising income), GasBuddy (falling income), YouNeedABudget
Social Trends Ageing population, diversity, millennials, mobile device growth, health focus, clean energy, urbanisation, personalisation Zipcar (urbanisation), Glaukos (ageing), CareZone (ageing + smartphone)
Technological Advances New technologies, emerging technologies, new uses for old technologies; technologies that spawn entire new industries Fitbit (wearables + health), OpenTable (restaurant reservations), Rokit Boost (smartphone accessories)
Political & Regulatory Changes New laws, deregulation, government data releases, industry-specific regulations Affordable Care Act → electronic health records startups; FAA drone regulations → drone industry
Two Caveats: (1) Distinguish trends from fads — startups rarely can ramp up fast enough to capitalise on a fad. (2) Trends are interconnected — smartphones succeeded because of economic, social, and technological trends converging simultaneously.

② Solving a Problem

Recognise problems people encounter in their daily lives — through observation, intuition, serendipity, or chance — and find a solution that can become a business.

Examples from the book
  • Jay Sorensen dropped a hot coffee in his lap → invented the insulating cup sleeve → Java Jacket (4 billion sleeves sold)
  • Women walking barefoot after nights in heels → CitySlips (portable flat shoes in a zip pouch)
  • Elderly people struggling with standard cell phones → GreatCall Jitterbug (large buttons, clear sound)
  • Jeannine Fradelizio — people forgetting which wine glass was theirs → Wine Glass Writer (sales: $1.5M in 2015)

Philip Kotler: "Every problem is a brilliantly disguised opportunity."

③ Finding Gaps in the Marketplace

Gaps exist when consumers need products/services that are not available, or not available to specific segments. Large retailers (Walmart, Costco) prioritise mainstream consumers, leaving gaps that specialty businesses fill.

Examples
  • Daisy Rock Guitars — no guitars made specifically for women
  • Southpaw Guitars — guitars only for left-handed players
  • Bodacious — stylish plus-size clothing that fits
  • Simply Gum — no healthy chewing gum on the market

Personal Characteristics That Aid Opportunity Recognition

Opportunity Recognition The process of perceiving the possibility of a profitable new business or a new product/service.

① Prior Industry Experience

Working in an industry helps spot underserved niches, builds credibility with investors/partners, and expands social contacts. However, outsiders can sometimes innovate precisely because they are not limited by "the way things are done" (e.g., Travis Kalanick/Uber — no transport experience; Elon Musk/Tesla — no automotive experience).

② Cognitive Factors — Entrepreneurial Alertness

Entrepreneurial Alertness The ability to notice things without engaging in deliberate search. A largely learned skill — people with more knowledge in an area tend to be more alert to opportunities in that area.

③ Social Networks

40–50% of new businesses are started from ideas gained through social contacts. People with extensive networks are exposed to more opportunities.

Tie TypeDescriptionImpact on Ideas
Strong-tieFrequent interaction (coworkers, spouses, close friends)Reinforce existing ideas — less likely to produce new ones
Weak-tieInfrequent interaction (casual acquaintances)More likely to spark brand-new ideas because the parties are not like-minded

④ Creativity

The process of generating a novel or useful idea. The creative process has five stages:

Stage 1
Preparation
Background knowledge, experience, skills the entrepreneur brings
Stage 2
Incubation
"Mulling it over" — conscious or unconscious processing
Stage 3
Insight ("Eureka!")
The flash of recognition — the opportunity is seen
Stage 4
Evaluation
Scrutinise the idea for viability — often skipped (a mistake)
Stage 5
Elaboration
Details worked out; business plan written; idea becomes value

Techniques for Generating Ideas

① Brainstorming

Generating a large quantity of ideas about a specific topic quickly. Can be informal (notepad) or formal (group sessions with a moderator).

Four rules for formal brainstorming:

  1. No criticism — no chuckles, raised eyebrows, or sceptical expressions. Criticism kills creativity.
  2. Freewheeling encouraged — more ideas = better. Even crazy ideas can spark good ones.
  3. Move quickly — capture the essence; don't slow down for neatness.
  4. Leapfrogging encouraged — use one idea to jump to others rapidly.
Mind Maps: A visual tool used alongside brainstorming. A central concept (e.g., "new innovative cooler") is placed at centre; branches show related ideas; sub-branches add detail. Tools: Bubble.us, MindMode, Mindomo.
Bug Reports: A technique where students list 50–75 things that "bug" them in daily life. The large number forces beyond the obvious (parking, food, roommates) and uncovers deeper problems that might become businesses.

② Focus Groups

A gathering of 5–10 people selected because of their relationship to the issue being discussed. Typically used as a follow-up to brainstorming — after a general idea is formed — to refine and test it.

Run by a trained moderator whose goals are to keep the group focused and generate lively discussion. The "give-and-take" dynamic can surface insights that surveys miss.

Example A coffee shop focus group revealed customers wanted 3–5 lb bags of specialty coffee (discounted) rather than just 1 lb bags — uncovering a new product line opportunity.

③ Library and Internet Research

Libraries provide industry magazines, trade journals, and expensive databases (e.g., IBISWorld). A reference librarian can guide research. Internet searches for "new business ideas" combined with Google Alerts on specific keywords, or following industry experts on Twitter using hashtags, can generate and sustain idea streams.

④ Other Techniques

  • Customer Advisory Boards — groups that regularly discuss needs, wants, and problems.
  • Day-in-the-Life Research — teams observe customers in their actual environments (e.g., Intuit visits users' homes/businesses).
Scenarios often ask you to recommend a method for generating ideas. Match the method to the situation: brainstorming for generating many rough ideas early; focus groups for refining a specific idea with real users; library research for validating market size and competition.
Chapter 4 — Developing an Effective Business Model

Business Models and Their Importance

What Is a Business Model?

Business Model A firm's plan or recipe for how it creates, delivers, and captures value for its stakeholders. It represents the core aspects of the business and describes how those aspects fit together and support one another.
Critical Idea

The quality of a business model and the quality of its execution together determine firm performance. The best business models are difficult for competitors to imitate. A firm wants its model to be uniquely different from competitors — the greater the difference (assuming effective development), the stronger the competitive advantage.

Three core questions every business model must answer:

  • Create value: What problem do we solve / what need do we fill?
  • Deliver value: How does the solution reach the customer?
  • Capture value: How does the firm make money from this?

Timing of business model development: After initial validation of the business idea but before fleshing out all operational details. Critically: founders should "get out of the building" and talk to potential customers as the model takes shape.

General Categories of Business Models

① Standard Business Models

Existing plans or recipes that firms use to create, deliver, and capture value. Many were developed offline and moved online (e.g., Amazon adapted the Sears Roebuck mail-order model).

Model NameDescriptionExamples
AdvertisingProvides advertisers access to targeted customer nichesGoogle, Facebook, Pinterest
AuctionPlatform for items sold in auction formateBay, uBid.com
Bricks and ClicksIntegrates offline (physical) and online presenceApple, Barnes & Noble, J. Crew
FranchiseLicences trademark and method of doing business to franchiseesPanera Bread, 24 Hour Fitness

Low-CostDrives down costs; serves large volumesSouthwest Airlines, Warby Parker, Costco

No model is inherently superior. Strength and weaknesses vary. Example: Subscription models have recurring revenue (strength) but suffer from "churn" — customers cancelling (weakness). A firm must still validate the opportunity and assess feasibility regardless of model type.

② Disruptive Business Models

Rare models that change the way business is conducted in an industry or important niche. Two types:

  • New Market Disruption: Addresses a market that previously was not served. Example: Google AdWords — made online advertising affordable for small businesses (previously minimum $10,000 ad buy was required).
  • Low-End Market Disruption: Enters when existing firms over-serve customers (too much quality for what customers need), allowing a simpler, cheaper, or more convenient alternative. Example: Southwest Airlines vs. legacy carriers; Uber vs. taxis.

The Barringer/Ireland Business Model Template

A visual framework of 4 categories and 12 elements that together describe a firm's entire business model. It is both a planning tool and an analysis tool — for scenarios, you fill it in based on the information provided.

CORE STRATEGY
RESOURCES
Business Mission
Why does the firm exist? What is its overarching purpose? Acts as financial and moral compass for all decisions.
Basis of Differentiation
What causes customers to choose this firm over competitors? (2–3 clear, benefit-focused points, not features)
Core Competency
Specific capabilities that support the business model and set it apart from rivals. Must be hard to imitate.
Key Assets
Physical, financial, intellectual, or human assets that enable the business model to function.
Target Market
The specific segment the firm focuses on. Start narrow — most firms do NOT start by selling to broad markets.
Product / Market Scope
The products and markets the firm will concentrate on initially and how scope will expand over 3–5 years.
Resources are inputs a firm uses to produce, sell, distribute & service its offering
FINANCIALS
OPERATIONS
Revenue Streams
How the firm makes money. Can have one or multiple streams. Common types: product sale, subscription, advertising, commissions, licensing, matchmaking, renting/leasing, download fee, service sale.
Product (or Service) Production
How products/services are produced — in-house, contract manufacturer, or outsource provider. For services: how the service is delivered.
Cost Structure
Most important costs. Identify: (1) cost-driven vs. value-driven; (2) fixed vs. variable cost nature; (3) major cost categories.
Financing / Funding
How the business model is funded. Three categories: capital costs, one-time expenses, ramp-up expenses (losses before profitability).
Channels
How products/services reach customers — direct (own website/store), intermediaries (wholesalers, distributors), or both.
Key Partners
Suppliers and other partners whose resources/capabilities the firm relies on. Start-ups rely heavily on partners because they can't do everything themselves.
All 12 Elements Must Fit Together

The power of the template lies in interdependence. A change in one element (e.g., target market) cascades through all others. Example: Birchbox's target market ("women excited to try new things") required specific core competencies, channels, and revenue streams that all aligned.

Deep Dive: Each of the 12 Elements

1. Business Mission — Should define the firm's reason for being, describe what makes it different, be challenging but achievable, convey passion, and be honest. Example: Zynga's mission: "Connecting the world through games."

2. Basis of Differentiation — Focus on benefits (what the product does for the customer), not features (technical specs). Example: ZUCA backpack on rollers — benefit: relieves back pain AND is sturdy enough to sit on. Limit to 2–3 clear points.

3. Target Market — Can be demographic OR psychographic. Birchbox: not age-based, but "women excited to try new things." Most new businesses start with a narrow niche, then expand.

4. Product/Market Scope — Start narrow; expand over time. Dropbox started with one product for Silicon Valley tech users (2008); by 2015 had 7 products for all internet users globally.

5. Core Competency — A specific, hard-to-imitate capability that makes the model work. Netflix's early model depended on supply chain management for DVD delivery. List 2–3 competencies max.

6. Key Assets — Can be physical (stores, equipment), intellectual (patents, brands), financial (cash, credit lines), or human (founders, employees, advisors). Airbnb's key assets are an app, its brand, and customer/partner relationships — NOT physical rooms.

7. Revenue Streams — Clearly identify all streams in bullet-point format. Multiple streams leverage existing customer value. Example: Birchbox has 3 streams: subscription, online store, physical store.

8. Cost Structure — Cost-driven businesses (Warby Parker) minimise costs throughout. Value-driven businesses (optometrists) focus on quality and personalised service. Know your fixed vs. variable cost ratio — it impacts how much capital you need upfront vs. ongoing.

9. Financing/Funding — Estimate (1) capital costs, (2) one-time launch expenses, (3) ramp-up expenses (losses before break-even). Most businesses need funding before generating revenue.

10. Product/Service Production — Be specific about where and how production happens. Not just "we'll use a contract manufacturer" — provide enough detail for the model to make sense.

11. Channels — Direct (website, owned stores), indirect (distributors, wholesalers), or hybrid. Channel choice affects pricing and margins. Warby Parker sells direct at $95 (must keep 100% margin). GoPro sells through multiple channels (can afford to share margins).

12. Key Partners — Suppliers (critical for operations), strategic alliances, joint ventures, consortia, freelancers. Advantages: resource access, risk/cost sharing, speed to market. Disadvantages: loss of proprietary information, management complexity, partial loss of decision autonomy.

Strategic Analysis Tools

SWOT & PESTEL Analysis

SWOT Analysis

SWOT is a strategic planning tool that evaluates a company's internal and external environment. It is commonly used in entrepreneurship to assess whether a business idea or scenario is viable.

Internal vs External

Internal factors (within the firm's control): Strengths, Weaknesses.
External factors (outside the firm's control): Opportunities, Threats.

S — Strengths (Internal)

What does the firm do well? What advantages does it have?

  • Unique skills, core competencies
  • Strong brand, loyal customers
  • Patented technology or processes
  • Cost advantages, efficient operations
  • Strong founding team / execution intelligence
W — Weaknesses (Internal)

What does the firm do poorly? What resources are lacking?

  • Limited capital or financing
  • Lack of brand recognition
  • Weak distribution channels
  • Missing skills in the founding team
  • Over-reliance on a single customer/supplier
O — Opportunities (External)

What external factors could be leveraged?

  • Emerging trends (technology, social, economic)
  • Gaps in the marketplace
  • Regulatory changes creating new demand
  • Competitors' weaknesses or exits
  • New markets opening up
T — Threats (External)

What external factors could harm the firm?

  • New competitors or disruptive models
  • Changing consumer preferences
  • Adverse regulatory changes
  • Economic downturns / rising costs
  • Technological obsolescence

How to Use SWOT in a Scenario Question

  1. Read the scenario carefully and extract facts.
  2. Categorise each fact: Is it about the firm (S/W) or the environment (O/T)?
  3. Is it positive for the firm (S/O) or negative (W/T)?
  4. Develop strategic implications: How can strengths exploit opportunities? How can weaknesses be addressed before threats materialise?
SWOT + Business Model Link: Strengths often map to Core Competencies and Key Assets. Weaknesses often reveal gaps in the business model. Opportunities correspond to gaps in the marketplace or trends to observe. Threats help assess competitive environment and risk in the Cost Structure and Revenue Streams.

PESTEL Analysis

PESTEL is a framework for analysing the macro-environmental factors that affect a business. It is particularly useful for identifying trends (Observing Trends — Chapter 2) and for understanding the external context in which a business operates.

P
Political

Government stability, trade policy, labour laws, foreign policy, tax policy. Political instability creates risks; stable governments attract investment.

Examples: new administration, trade tariffs, subsidies for green tech.

E
Economic

GDP growth, inflation, exchange rates, interest rates, unemployment, disposable income, consumer spending patterns.

Rising incomes → discretionary spending ↑. Rising interest rates → borrowing costs ↑.

S
Social

Demographics, cultural attitudes, lifestyle changes, health consciousness, education levels, population growth rates.

Ageing population → health care demand ↑. Urbanisation → car-sharing demand ↑.

T
Technological

R&D activity, automation, innovation, technology transfer, internet penetration, mobile connectivity, emerging technologies.

Smartphones → new industries. AI → disruption of existing services.

E
Environmental

Climate change, weather, environmental regulations, sustainability requirements, carbon footprint, green energy policies.

Carbon taxes → clean energy startups. Consumer eco-consciousness → green products.

L
Legal

Discrimination laws, consumer protection, antitrust laws, employment laws, health and safety regulations, intellectual property law.

GDPR → data privacy compliance. Patent law → protection of innovations.

PESTEL → Opportunities & Threats

PESTEL factors feed directly into the Opportunities and Threats sections of SWOT. Political/Legal factors = regulatory opportunities or threats. Economic factors = market size and customer purchasing power. Social factors = demand trends. Technological factors = disruption risk and innovation opportunities. Environmental factors = growing ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) requirements.

How to Use PESTEL in a Scenario Question

  1. Scan the scenario for macro-environmental information.
  2. Tag each piece of information with a PESTEL category.
  3. Assess: Does this factor create an opportunity or a threat for the business described?
  4. Connect PESTEL insights to the Three Ways to Identify Opportunities (Ch. 2) — particularly Observing Trends.
  5. Use PESTEL findings to populate the external context when analysing or filling out the Barringer/Ireland Business Model Template.
Exam Strategy

How to Analyse a Business Scenario

Most exam questions will present a short business scenario and ask you to apply concepts. Here is a step-by-step framework:

Step-by-Step Scenario Analysis Framework
1
Identify What Kind of Business This Is Salary-substitute, lifestyle, or entrepreneurial firm? If entrepreneurial, is the model standard or disruptive? This frames everything.
2
Classify the Motivation (Ch. 1) Why is this person starting the business? Own boss? Pursue an idea? Financial rewards? This reveals their likely passion level and risk tolerance.
3
Identify the Opportunity (Ch. 2) Is this truly an opportunity (attractive, timely, durable, value-creating) or just an idea? Which of the three sources does it come from: observing trends, solving a problem, or finding a gap? What environmental trends support it (PESTEL)?
4
Conduct SWOT Analysis Map the scenario's facts to Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. This provides a structured view of viability.
5
Fill the Barringer/Ireland Business Model Template (Ch. 4) Use information from the scenario to populate all 12 elements. For elements not stated, use your own knowledge to suggest what they should be. Check internal consistency — do the elements fit together?
6
Evaluate the Entrepreneur (Ch. 1) Do they show passion, product/customer focus, tenacity, and execution intelligence? Identify any myths they may be operating under.
7
Recommend Idea-Generation Techniques (if asked) Use brainstorming, focus groups, or library/internet research depending on what stage the business is at and what information is needed.

Scenario Red Flags (Signs of a Bad Business)

  • Founder passionate about entrepreneurship but not about the specific business (like Prim's founders)
  • Idea but no real opportunity — no identifiable market need
  • Window of opportunity already closed (late entrant into saturated market)
  • Business model elements don't fit together (e.g., premium target market but cost-driven model)
  • No basis of differentiation — "slightly better version" of what exists
  • Over-reliance on a fragile partnership (verbal agreements vs. written contracts — like Prim's laundry partners)
  • No plan for funding ramp-up period (business needs cash before it generates revenue)

Scenario Green Flags (Signs of a Promising Business)

  • Founder has prior industry experience AND strong social networks
  • Opportunity is externally validated (multiple trends converging)
  • Clear basis of differentiation based on benefits, not features
  • Niche target market that is underserved
  • Business model elements are mutually reinforcing and hard to imitate
  • Multiple revenue streams that are additive, not dilutive
  • Business launches narrow, with a clear expansion roadmap

Opportunity RecognitionPerceiving the possibility of a profitable new business or product/service.
Entrepreneurial AlertnessThe ability to notice things without deliberate search — largely a learned skill.
Strong-Tie RelationshipsFrequent interactions (friends, coworkers); tend to reinforce existing ideas.
Weak-Tie RelationshipsInfrequent interactions (casual acquaintances); more likely to spark new ideas.
BrainstormingTechnique for quickly generating many ideas; no criticism allowed.
Mind MapVisual diagram organising ideas hierarchically around a central concept.
Bug ReportList of 50–75 things that "bug" someone in daily life — used to find problems that could become businesses.
Focus Group5–10 selected people discussing a topic to generate/refine ideas.
Network EntrepreneursThose who find business ideas through social contacts (vs. solo entrepreneurs who find them alone).
Business ModelA firm's plan for how it creates, delivers, and captures value for stakeholders.
Standard Business ModelExisting plans firms use — advertising, auction, subscription, freemium, etc.
Disruptive Business ModelA model that changes the way business is conducted in an industry.
Core CompetencyA specific, hard-to-imitate capability that supports the business model.
Basis of DifferentiationWhat causes consumers to choose this firm over competitors (benefit-focused).
Revenue StreamA specific way a firm earns money (product sale, subscription, advertising, etc.).
Cost StructureThe most important costs a firm incurs to support its business model.
Creative DestructionSchumpeter's concept: entrepreneurs create new products/technologies that make existing ones obsolete, stimulating growth.
SWOTStrengths, Weaknesses (internal); Opportunities, Threats (external).
PESTELPolitical, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal — macro-environment analysis.
